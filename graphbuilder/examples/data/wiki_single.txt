'''Calorimetry''' is the science or act of measuring changes in parameters of [[chemical reaction]]s, [[physical change]]s and [[phase transition]]s, for the purpose of deriving the [[heat]] or [[heat transfer]] associated with those changes.   Calorimetry is performed with a [[calorimeter]]. The word ''calorimetry'' is derived from the Latin word ''calor'', meaning heat and the Greek word ''μέτρον'' (metron), meaning measure.  Scottish physician and scientist [[Joseph Black]], who was the first to recognize the distinction between [[heat]] and [[temperature]], is said to be the founder of the science of calorimetry.&lt;ref name=&quot;Laider&quot; &gt;{{cite book|author= [[Keith J. Laidler|Laidler, Keith, J.]]|title=The World of Physical Chemistry|publisher=Oxford University Press|year=1993|isbn=0-19-855919-4}}&lt;/ref&gt; 

'''Indirect calorimetry''' calculates [[heat]] that living organisms produce by measuring either their production of [[carbon dioxide]] and nitrogen waste (frequently [[ammonia]] in aquatic organisms, or [[urea]] in terrestrial ones), or from their consumption of [[oxygen]]. 
[[Antoine Lavoisier|Lavoisier]] noted in 1780 that heat production can be predicted from oxygen consumption this way, using [[multiple regression]]. The [[Dynamic Energy Budget]] theory explains why this procedure is correct. Heat generated by living organisms may also be measured by '''direct calorimetry''', in which the entire organism is placed inside the calorimeter for the measurement.

A widely used modern instrument is the '''[[differential scanning calorimeter]]''', a device which allows thermal data to be obtained on small amounts of material. It involves heating the sample at a controlled rate and recording the heat flow either into or from the specimen.

==Classical calorimetric calculation of heat==

===Basic classical calculation with respect to volume===

Calorimetry requires  that the material being heated have known definite thermal constitutive properties. The classical rule, recognized by [[Rudolf Clausius|Clausius]] and by [[William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin|Kelvin]], is that the pressure exerted by the calorimetric material is fully and rapidly determined solely by its temperature and volume; this rule is for changes that do not involve phase change, such as melting of ice. There are many materials that do not comply with this rule, and for them, the present formula of classical calorimetry does not provide an adequate account. Here the classical rule is assumed to hold for the calorimetric material being used, and the propositions are mathematically written:

The thermal response of the calorimetric material is fully described by its pressure &lt;math&gt;p\ &lt;/math&gt; as the value of its constitutive function &lt;math&gt;p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; of just the volume &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; and the temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;. All increments are here required to be very small.

When a small increment of heat is gained by a calorimetric body, with small increments, &lt;math&gt;\delta V\ &lt;/math&gt; of its volume, and &lt;math&gt;\delta T\ &lt;/math&gt; of its temperature, the increment of heat, &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt;, gained by the body of calorimetric material, is given by

:&lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ =C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\, \delta V\,+\,C^{(T)}_V(V,T)\,\delta T&lt;/math&gt;

where

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the latent heat with respect to volume, of the calorimetric material at constant temperature, while the pressure and volume of the material are allowed to vary freely, at volume &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; and temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;.
:&lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_V(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the heat capacity, of the calorimetric material at constant volume, while the pressure and temperature of the material are allowed to vary freely, at volume &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; and temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;. It is customary to write &lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_V(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; simply as &lt;math&gt;C_V(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, or even more briefly as &lt;math&gt;C_V\ &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;Bryan 1907 21–22&quot;&gt;Bryan, G.H. (1907), pages 21–22.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Partington, J.R. (1949), pages 155–157.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Prigogine, I., Defay, R. (1950/1954). ''Chemical Thermodynamics'', Longmans, Green &amp; Co, London, pages 22-23.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Crawford, F.H. (1963), Section 5.9, pp. 120–121.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 3.6&quot;&gt;Adkins, C.J. (1975), Section 3.6, pages 43-46.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), pages 20-21.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Landsberg, P.T. (1978), page 11.&lt;/ref&gt;

The latent heat with respect to volume is the heat required for unit increment in volume at constant temperature. It can be said to be 'measured along an isotherm', and the pressure the material exerts is allowed to vary freely, according to its constitutive law &lt;math&gt;p=p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;. For a given material, it can have a positive or negative sign or exceptionally it can be zero, and this can depend on the temperature, as it does for water about 4 C.&lt;ref&gt;Maxwell, J.C. (1872), pages 232-233.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Lewis, G.N., Randall, M. (1923/1961), pages 378-379.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), pages 9-10, 15-18, 36-37.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C.A. (1980). ''The Tragicomical History of Thermodynamics, 1822-1854'', Springer, New York, ISBN 0-387-90403-4.&lt;/ref&gt; The concept of latent heat with respect to volume was perhaps first recognized by [[Joseph Black]] in 1762.&lt;ref&gt;Lewis, G.N., Randall, M. (1923/1961), page 29.&lt;/ref&gt; The term 'latent heat of expansion' is also used.&lt;ref&gt;Maxwell, J.C. (1872), page 73.&lt;/ref&gt; The latent heat with respect to volume can also be called the 'latent energy with respect to volume'. For all of these usages of 'latent heat', a more systematic terminology uses 'latent heat capacity'.

The heat capacity at constant volume is the heat required for unit increment in temperature at constant volume. It can be said to be 'measured along an isochor', and again, the pressure the material exerts is allowed to vary freely. It always has a positive sign. This means that for an increase in the temperature of a body without change of its volume, heat must be supplied to it. This is consistent with common experience.

Quantities like &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt; are sometimes called 'curve differentials', because they are measured along curves in the &lt;math&gt;(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; surface.

===Constant-volume calorimetry (Bomb Calorimetry)===
Constant-volume calorimetry is calorimetry performed at a constant [[volume]]. This involves the use of a [[constant-volume calorimeter]]. Heat is still measured by the above-stated principle of calorimetry.

This means that in a suitably constructed calorimeter, the increment of volume &lt;math&gt;\delta V\ &lt;/math&gt; can be made to vanish, &lt;math&gt;\delta V=0\ &lt;/math&gt;. For constant-volume calorimetry:

:&lt;math&gt;\delta Q = C_V \delta T\ &lt;/math&gt;

where

:&lt;math&gt;\delta T\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the increment in [[temperature]] and
:&lt;math&gt;C_V\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the [[heat capacity]] at constant volume.

===Classical heat calculation with respect to pressure===

From the above rule of calculation of heat with respect to volume, there follows one with respect to pressure.&lt;ref name=&quot;Bryan 1907 21–22&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 3.6&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Crawford, F.H. (1963), Section 5.10, pp. 121–122.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;TB 1977 23&quot;&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 23.&lt;/ref&gt;

In a process of small increments, &lt;math&gt;\delta p\ &lt;/math&gt; of its pressure, and &lt;math&gt;\delta T\ &lt;/math&gt; of its temperature, the increment of heat, &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt;, gained by the body of calorimetric material, is given by

:&lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ =C^{(p)}_T(p,T)\, \delta p\,+\,C^{(T)}_p(p,T)\,\delta T&lt;/math&gt;

where

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(p)}_T(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the latent heat with respect to pressure, of the calorimetric material at constant temperature, while the volume and pressure of the body are allowed to vary freely, at pressure &lt;math&gt;p\ &lt;/math&gt; and temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;;
:&lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_p(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the heat capacity, of the calorimetric material at constant pressure, while the temperature and volume of the body are allowed to vary freely, at pressure &lt;math&gt;p\ &lt;/math&gt; and temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;. It is customary to write &lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_p(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; simply as &lt;math&gt;C_p(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, or even more briefly as &lt;math&gt;C_p\ &lt;/math&gt;.

The new quantities here are related to the previous ones:&lt;ref name=&quot;Bryan 1907 21–22&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 3.6&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;TB 1977 23&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Crawford, F.H. (1963), Section 5.11, pp. 123–124.&lt;/ref&gt;

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(p)}_T(p,T)=\frac{C^{(V)}_T(V,T)}{\left.\cfrac{\partial p}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}} &lt;/math&gt;

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_p(p,T)=C^{(T)}_V(V,T)-C^{(V)}_T(V,T) \frac{\left.\cfrac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)}}{\left.\cfrac{\partial p}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}} &lt;/math&gt;
where
:&lt;math&gt;\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}&lt;/math&gt; denotes the [[partial derivative]] of &lt;math&gt;p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; with respect to &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; evaluated for &lt;math&gt;(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;

and

:&lt;math&gt;\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)}&lt;/math&gt; denotes the partial derivative of &lt;math&gt;p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; with respect to &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt; evaluated for &lt;math&gt;(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;.

The latent heats &lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;C^{(p)}_T(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; are always of opposite sign.&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 24.&lt;/ref&gt;

It is common to refer to the ratio of specific heats as

:&lt;math&gt;\gamma(V,T)=\frac{C^{(T)}_p(p,T)}{C^{(T)}_V(V,T)}&lt;/math&gt; often just written as &lt;math&gt;\gamma=\frac{C_p}{C_V}&lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 25.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Kondepudi, D. (2008), pages 66-67.&lt;/ref&gt;

===Calorimetry through phase change===

An early calorimeter was that used by [[Pierre-Simon Laplace|Laplace]] and [[Antoine Lavoisier|Lavoisier]], as shown in the figure above. It worked at constant temperature, and at atmospheric pressure. The latent heat involved was then not a latent heat with respect to volume or with respect to pressure, as in the above account for calorimetry without phase change. The latent heat involved in this calorimeter was with respect to phase change, naturally occurring at constant temperature. This kind of calorimeter worked by measurement of mass of water produced by the melting of ice, which is a [[phase transition|phase change]].

===Cumulation of heating===

For a time-dependent process of heating of the calorimetric material, defined by a continuous joint progression &lt;math&gt;P(t_1,t_2)\ &lt;/math&gt; of &lt;math&gt;V(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;T(t)\ &lt;/math&gt;, starting at time &lt;math&gt;t_1\ &lt;/math&gt; and ending at time &lt;math&gt;t_2\ &lt;/math&gt;, there can be calculated an accumulated quantity of heat delivered, &lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(P(t_1,t_2))\, &lt;/math&gt; . This calculation is done by [[line integral|mathematical integration along the progression]] with respect to time. This is because increments of heat are 'additive'; but this does not mean that heat is a conservative quantity. The idea that heat was a conservative quantity was invented by [[Antoine Lavoisier|Lavoisier]], and is called the '[[caloric theory]]'; by the middle of the nineteenth century it was recognized as mistaken. Written with the symbol &lt;math&gt;\Delta\ &lt;/math&gt;, the quantity &lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(P(t_1,t_2))\, &lt;/math&gt; is not at all restricted to be an increment with very small values; this is in contrast with &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt;.

One can write

:&lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(P(t_1,t_2))\ &lt;/math&gt;

::&lt;math&gt;=\int_{P(t_1,t_2)} \dot Q(t)dt&lt;/math&gt;

::&lt;math&gt;=\int_{P(t_1,t_2)} C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\, \dot V(t)\, dt\,+\,\int_{P(t_1,t_2)}C^{(T)}_V(V,T)\,\dot T(t)\,dt &lt;/math&gt;.

This expression uses quantities such as &lt;math&gt;\dot Q(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; which are defined in the section below headed 'Mathematical aspects of the above rules'.

===Mathematical aspects of the above rules===

The use of 'very small' quantities such as &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt; is related to the physical requirement for the quantity &lt;math&gt;p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; to be 'rapidly determined' by &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt;; such 'rapid determination' refers to a physical process. These 'very small' quantities are used in the [[Gottfried Leibniz|Leibniz]] approach to the [[infinitesimal calculus]]. The [[Isaac Newton|Newton]] approach uses instead '[[Method of Fluxions|fluxions]]' such as &lt;math&gt;\dot V(t) = \left.\frac{dV}{dt}\right|_t&lt;/math&gt;, which makes it more obvious that &lt;math&gt;p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; must be 'rapidly determined'.

In terms of fluxions, the above first rule of calculation can be written&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 20.&lt;/ref&gt;

:&lt;math&gt;\dot Q(t)\ =C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\, \dot V(t)\,+\,C^{(T)}_V(V,T)\,\dot T(t)&lt;/math&gt;

where

:&lt;math&gt;t\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the time

:&lt;math&gt;\dot Q(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the time rate of heating of the calorimetric material at time &lt;math&gt;t\ &lt;/math&gt;

:&lt;math&gt;\dot V(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the time rate of change of volume of the calorimetric material at time &lt;math&gt;t\ &lt;/math&gt;

:&lt;math&gt;\dot T(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; denotes the time rate of change of temperature of the calorimetric material.

The increment &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt; and the fluxion &lt;math&gt;\dot Q(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; are obtained for a particular time &lt;math&gt;t\ &lt;/math&gt; that determines the values of the quantities on the righthand sides of the above rules. But this is not a reason to expect that there should exist a [[Function (mathematics)|mathematical function]] &lt;math&gt;Q(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;. For this reason, the increment &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt; is said to be an 'imperfect differential' or an '[[inexact differential]]'.&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 1975 16&quot;&gt;Adkins, C.J. (1975), Section 1.9.3, page 16.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Landsberg, P.T. (1978), pages 8-9.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;An account of this is given by Landsberg, P.T. (1978), Chapter 4, pages 26-33.&lt;/ref&gt; Some books indicate this by writing &lt;math&gt;q\ &lt;/math&gt; instead of &lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref&gt;Fowler, R., Guggenheim, E.A. (1939/1965). ''Statistical Thermodynamics. A version of Statistical Mechanics for Students of Physics and Chemistry'', Cambridge University Press, Cambridge UK, page 57.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Guggenheim, E.A. (1949/1967), Section 1.10, pages 9-11.&lt;/ref&gt; Also, the notation ''đQ'' is used in some books.&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 1975 16&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Lebon Jou Casas-Vázquez 2008&quot;&gt;Lebon, G., Jou, D., Casas-Vázquez, J. (2008). ''Understanding Non-equilibrium Thermodynamics: Foundations, Applications, Frontiers'', Springer-Verlag, Berlin, e-ISBN 978-3-540-74252-4, page 7.&lt;/ref&gt; Carelessness about this can lead to error.&lt;ref name=&quot;Planck 1923/1926 57&quot;&gt;Planck, M. (1923/1926), page 57.&lt;/ref&gt;

The quantity &lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(P(t_1,t_2))\ &lt;/math&gt; is properly said to be a [[Functional (mathematics)|functional]] of the continuous joint progression &lt;math&gt;P(t_1,t_2)\ &lt;/math&gt; of &lt;math&gt;V(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;T(t)\ &lt;/math&gt;, but, in the mathematical definition of a [[Function (mathematics)|function]], &lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(P(t_1,t_2))\ &lt;/math&gt; is not a function of &lt;math&gt;(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;. Although the fluxion &lt;math&gt;\dot Q(t)\ &lt;/math&gt; is defined here as a function of time &lt;math&gt;t\ &lt;/math&gt;, the symbols &lt;math&gt;Q\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;Q(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; respectively standing alone are not defined here.

===Physical scope of the above rules of calorimetry===

The above rules refer only to suitable calorimetric materials. The terms 'rapidly' and 'very small' call for empirical physical checking of the domain of validity of the above rules.

The above rules for the calculation of heat belong to pure calorimetry. They make no reference to [[thermodynamics]], and were mostly understood before the advent of thermodynamics. They are the basis of the 'thermo' contribution to thermodynamics. The 'dynamics' contribution is based on the idea of [[thermodynamic work|work]], which is not used in the above rules of calculation.

==Experimentally conveniently measured coefficients==

Empirically, it is convenient to measure properties of calorimetric materials under experimentally controlled conditions.

===Pressure increase at constant volume===

For measurements at experimentally controlled volume, one can use the assumption, stated above, that the pressure of the body of calorimetric material is can be expressed as a function of its volume and temperature.

For measurement at constant experimentally controlled volume, the isochoric coefficient of pressure rise with temperature, is defined by

:&lt;math&gt;\alpha _V(V,T)\ = \frac{\left.\cfrac{\partial p}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}}{p(V,T)} &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;IG 46&quot;&gt;Iribarne, J.V., Godson, W.L. (1973/1981), page 46.&lt;/ref&gt;

===Expansion at constant pressure===

For measurements at experimentally controlled pressure, it is assumed that the volume &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; of the body of calorimetric material can be expressed as a function &lt;math&gt;V(T,p)\ &lt;/math&gt; of its temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt; and pressure &lt;math&gt;p\ &lt;/math&gt;. This assumption is related to, but is not the same as, the above used assumption that the pressure of the body of calorimetric material is known as a function of its volume and temperature; anomalous behaviour of materials can affect this relation.

The quantity that is conveniently measured at constant experimentally controlled pressure, the isobaric volume expansion coefficient, is defined by

:&lt;math&gt;\beta _p(T,p)\ = \frac{\left.\cfrac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right|_{(T,p)}}{V(T,p)} &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;IG 46&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;LR 54&quot;&gt;Lewis, G.N., Randall, M. (1923/1961), page 54.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Guggenheim 38&quot;&gt;Guggenheim, E.A. (1949/1967), page 38.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Callen 84&quot;&gt;Callen, H.B. (1960/1985), page 84.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 38&quot;&gt;Adkins, C.J. (1975), page 38.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Bailyn 49&quot;&gt;Bailyn, M. (1994), page 49.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Kondepudi 180&quot;&gt;Kondepudi, D. (2008), page 180.&lt;/ref&gt;

===Compressibility at constant temperature===

For measurements at experimentally controlled temperature, it is again assumed that the volume &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; of the body of calorimetric material can be expressed as a function &lt;math&gt;V(T,p)\ &lt;/math&gt; of its temperature &lt;math&gt;T\ &lt;/math&gt; and pressure &lt;math&gt;p\ &lt;/math&gt;, with the same provisos as mentioned just above.

The quantity that is conveniently measured at constant experimentally controlled temperature, the isothermal compressibility, is defined by

:&lt;math&gt;\kappa _T(T,p)\ = -\frac{\left.\cfrac{\partial V}{\partial p}\right|_{(T,p)}}{V(T,p)} &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;LR 54&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Guggenheim 38&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Callen 84&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 38&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Bailyn 49&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Kondepudi 180&quot;/&gt;

==Relation between classical calorimetric quantities==

Assuming that the rule &lt;math&gt;p=p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; is known, one can derive the function &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\ &lt;/math&gt; that is used above in the classical heat calculation with respect to pressure. This function can be found experimentally from the coefficients &lt;math&gt;\beta _p(T,p)\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;\kappa _T(T,p)\ &lt;/math&gt; through the mathematically deducible relation

:&lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}=\frac{\beta _p(T,p)}{\kappa _T(T,p)}&lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;Kondepudi 181&quot;&gt;Kondepudi, D. (2008), page 181.&lt;/ref&gt;

==Connection between calorimetry and thermodynamics==

[[Thermodynamics]] developed gradually over the first half of the nineteenth century, building on the above theory of calorimetry which had been worked out before it, and on other discoveries. According to Gislason and Craig (2005): &quot;Most thermodynamic data come from calorimetry...&quot;&lt;ref&gt;Gislason, E.A., Craig, N.C. (2005). Cementing the foundations of thermodynamics:comparison of system-based and surroundings-based definitions of work and heat, ''J. Chem. Thermodynamics'' '''37''': 954-966.&lt;/ref&gt; According to Kondepudi (2008): &quot;Calorimetry is widely used in present day laboratories.&quot;&lt;ref&gt;Kondepudi, D. (2008), page 63.&lt;/ref&gt;

In terms of thermodynamics, the [[internal energy]] &lt;math&gt;U\ &lt;/math&gt; of the calorimetric material can be considered as the value of a function &lt;math&gt;U(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; of &lt;math&gt;(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, with partial derivatives &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\ &lt;/math&gt;.

Then it can be shown that one can write a thermodynamic version of the above calorimetric rules:

:&lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ =\left [p(V,T)\,+\,\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}\right ]\, \delta V\,+\,\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)}\,\delta T&lt;/math&gt;

with

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)=p(V,T)\,+\,\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}\ &lt;/math&gt;

and

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_V(V,T)=\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)}\ &lt;/math&gt; .&lt;ref name=&quot;Planck 1923/1926 57&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Preston, T. (1894/1904). ''The Theory of Heat'', second edition, revised by J.R. Cotter, Macmillan, London, pages 700-701.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Adkins, C.J. (1975), page 45.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 134.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Kondepudi, D. (2008), page 64.&lt;/ref&gt;

Again, further in terms of thermodynamics, the [[internal energy]] &lt;math&gt;U\ &lt;/math&gt; of the calorimetric material can sometimes, depending on the calorimetric material, be considered as the value of a function &lt;math&gt;U(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; of &lt;math&gt;(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, with partial derivatives &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial U}{\partial p}\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\ &lt;/math&gt;, and with &lt;math&gt;V\ &lt;/math&gt; being expressible as the value of a function &lt;math&gt;V(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; of &lt;math&gt;(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, with partial derivatives &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial V}{\partial p}\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\ &lt;/math&gt; .

Then, according to Adkins (1975),&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 46&quot;&gt;Adkins, C.J. (1975), page 46.&lt;/ref&gt; it can be shown that one can write a further thermodynamic version of the above calorimetric rules:

:&lt;math&gt;\delta Q\ =\left [\left. \frac{\partial U}{\partial p}\right |_{(p,T)}\,+\,p \left.\frac{\partial V}{\partial p}\right |_{(p,T)}\right ]\delta p\,+\,\left [ \left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right|_{(p,T)}\,+\,p \left.\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right |_{(p,T)}\right ]\delta T&lt;/math&gt;

with

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(p)}_T(p,T)=\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial p}\right|_{(p,T)}\,+\,p\left.\frac{\partial V}{\partial p}\right|_{(p,T)}\ &lt;/math&gt;

and

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(T)}_p(p,T)=\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right|_{(p,T)}\,+\,p\left.\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right|_{(p,T)}\ &lt;/math&gt; .&lt;ref name=&quot;Adkins 46&quot;/&gt;

Beyond the calorimetric fact noted above that the latent heats &lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; and &lt;math&gt;C^{(p)}_T(p,T)\ &lt;/math&gt; are always of opposite sign, it may be shown, using the thermodynamic concept of work, that also

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\,\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)} \geq 0\,.&lt;/math&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 59.&lt;/ref&gt;

==Special interest of thermodynamics in calorimetry: the isothermal segments of a Carnot cycle==

Calorimetry has a special benefit for thermodynamics. It tells about the heat absorbed or emitted in the isothermal segment of a [[Carnot cycle]].

A Carnot cycle is a special kind of cyclic process affecting a body composed of material suitable for use in a heat engine. Such a material is of the kind considered in calorimetry, as noted above, that exerts a pressure that is very rapidly determined just by temperature and volume. Such a body is said to change reversibly. A Carnot cycle consists of four successive stages or segments:

(1) a change in volume from a volume &lt;math&gt;V_a\ &lt;/math&gt; to a volume &lt;math&gt;V_b\ &lt;/math&gt; at constant temperature &lt;math&gt;T^+\ &lt;/math&gt; so as to incur a flow of heat into the body (known as an isothermal change)

(2) a change in volume from &lt;math&gt;V_b\ &lt;/math&gt; to a volume &lt;math&gt;V_c\ &lt;/math&gt; at a variable temperature just such as to incur no flow of heat (known as an adiabatic change)

(3) another isothermal change in volume from &lt;math&gt;V_c\ &lt;/math&gt; to a volume &lt;math&gt;V_d\ &lt;/math&gt; at constant temperature &lt;math&gt;T^-\ &lt;/math&gt; such as to incur a flow or heat out of the body and just such as to precisely prepare for the following change

(4) another adiabatic change of volume from &lt;math&gt;V_d\ &lt;/math&gt; back to &lt;math&gt;V_a\ &lt;/math&gt; just such as to return the body to its starting temperature &lt;math&gt;T^+\ &lt;/math&gt;.

In isothermal segment (1), the heat that flows into the body is given by

:&amp;nbsp;&amp;nbsp;&amp;nbsp;&lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(V_a,V_b;T^+)\,=\,\,\,\,\,\,\,\,\int_{V_a}^{V_b} C^{(V)}_T(V,T^+)\, dV\ &lt;/math&gt;

and in isothermal segment (3) the heat that flows out of the body is given by

:&lt;math&gt;-\Delta Q(V_c,V_d;T^-)\,=\,-\int_{V_c}^{V_d} C^{(V)}_T(V,T^-)\, dV\ &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), pages 52-53.&lt;/ref&gt;

Because the segments (2) and (4) are adiabats, no heat flows into or out of the body during them, and consequently the net heat supplied to the body during the cycle is given by

:&lt;math&gt;\Delta Q(V_a,V_b;T^+;V_c,V_d;T^-)\,=\,\Delta Q(V_a,V_b;T^+)\,+\,\Delta Q(V_c,V_d;T^-)\,=\,\int_{V_a}^{V_b} C^{(V)}_T(V,T^+)\, dV\,+\,\int_{V_c}^{V_d} C^{(V)}_T(V,T^-)\, dV\ &lt;/math&gt;.

This quantity is used by thermodynamics and is related in a special way to the net [[Work (thermodynamics)|work]] done by the body during the Carnot cycle. The net change of the body's internal energy during the Carnot cycle, &lt;math&gt;\Delta U(V_a,V_b;T^+;V_c,V_d;T^-)\ &lt;/math&gt;, is equal to zero, because the material of the working body has the special properties noted above.

==Special interest of calorimetry in thermodynamics: relations between classical calorimetric quantities==

===Relation of latent heat with respect to volume, and the equation of state===

The quantity &lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;, the latent heat with respect to volume, belongs to classical calorimetry. It accounts for the occurrence of energy transfer by work in a process in which heat is also transferred; the quantity, however, was considered before the relation between heat and work transfers was clarified by the invention of thermodynamics. In the light of thermodynamics, the classical calorimetric quantity is revealed as being tightly linked to the calorimetric material's equation of state &lt;math&gt;p=p(V,T)\ &lt;/math&gt;. Provided that the temperature &lt;math&gt;T\, &lt;/math&gt; is measured in the thermodynamic absolute scale, the relation is expressed in the formula

:&lt;math&gt;C^{(V)}_T(V,T)=T \left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right|_{(V,T)}\ &lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref&gt;Truesdell, C., Bharatha, S. (1977), page 150.&lt;/ref&gt;

===Difference of specific heats===

Advanced thermodynamics provides the relation

:&lt;math&gt;C_p(p,T)-C_V(V,T)=\left [p(V,T)\,+\,\left.\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right|_{(V,T)}\right ]\, \left.\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right|_{(p,T)}&lt;/math&gt;.

From this, further mathematical and thermodynamic reasoning leads to another relation between classical calorimetric quantities. The difference of specific heats is given by

:&lt;math&gt;C_p(p,T)-C_V(V,T)=\frac{TV\,\beta _p^2(T,p)}{\kappa _T(T,p)}&lt;/math&gt;.&lt;ref name=&quot;LR 54&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;Kondepudi 181&quot;/&gt;&lt;ref&gt;Callen, H.B. (1960/1985), page 86.&lt;/ref&gt;
